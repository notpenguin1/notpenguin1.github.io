Modular Multiplicative Inverse

A modular multiplicative inverse of an integer $a$ with respect to modulus $m$ is an integer $x$ such that $ax \equiv 1 \pmod m$.

Existence of MMI

A MMI exists iff $a$ and $m$ are coprime.

If $a$ and $m$ are coprime, consider $\{0a, 1a, 2a, \dots, (m-1)a\}$. If $\exists i \ne j: ia \equiv ij \pmod m$, then $m \mid a(i - j) \implies m \mid (i-j) \implies i = j$, which is a contradiction. Thus, $\{0a, 1a, 2a, \dots, (m-1)a\} = \{0, 1, \dots, m-1\}$, which means $\exists x: ax \equiv 1 \pmod m$.

If a MMI exists, then assume $(a, m) = k, k > 1$. Then $ax \equiv 1 \pmod m$. $\because k \mid m, ax \therefore k \mid 1 \implies k = 1$, contradiction. Thus, $(a, m) = 1$.

Multiplicative Group

The multiplicative group of integers modulo $n$ is a group of all non-negative integers less than $n$ that are coprime to $n$. This group can be denoted: $(\textrm{Z}/n\textrm{Z})^\times, (\textrm{Z}/n\textrm{Z})^*$ or $Z_n^*$

$Z_n^*$ is closed under multiplication: $\forall a, b \in Z_n^*: ab \equiv c \pmod n$ where $c \in Z_n^*$. (Since $a, b$ are coprime with $n$, $ab$ must also be coprime).

Euler’s Theorem

Euler’s totient function, $\phi(n)$, returns the number of positive integers less than $n$ that are coprime to $n$.

Euler’s theorem states that for all integers $a$ coprime to $n$:

$$a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$$

Proof of Euler’s Theorem

Consider $Z_n^* = \{r_1, r_2, \dots, r_{\phi(n)}\}$. Let $a \in Z_n^*$.

Assume $\exists i \ne j: ar_i \equiv ar_j \pmod n$. Then $n \mid a(r_i - r_j) \implies n \mid r_i - r_j \implies r_i = r_j$, contradiction. Thus, $ar_i \not\equiv ar_j \pmod n$.

Furthermore $ar_i \in Z_n^*$ (since $Z_n^*$ is closed under multiplication). $\{ar_1, ar_2, \dots ar_{\phi(n)}\} = Z_n^* \implies r_1 r_2 \dots r_{\phi(n)} \equiv ar_1 ar_2 \dots ar_{\phi(n)} \pmod n \implies a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

Primitive Root

An integer $g$ is a primitive root modulo $n$ if every integer $a$ coprime to $n$ is congruent to a power of $g$ modulo $n$.

$$\forall a \in \textrm{Z}, (a, n) = 1, \exists k \in \textrm{Z}: a \equiv g^k \pmod n$$

$k$ is known as the index or discrete logarithm of $a$ to base $g$ modulo $n$. Finding $k$ is the discrete logarithm problem, and it is not known whether a polynomial algorithm exists to find $k$ given $a, g, n$.

Elliptic Curve

An elliptic curve is a plane curve defined by the equation $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$. The discriminant of an elliptic curve, $\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2)$, must not be zero, as an elliptic curve is non-singular.

Operations

There is a point at infinity, represented by $0$, which is the identity element.

Consider 2 distinct points $P, Q$. If there is no intersection between the straight line through $P, Q$ and the ellipse, then $P + Q = 0$ and $P = -Q$, where the negation of a point is the point with the same x-coordinate but negative y-coordinate. Otherwise, $P + Q = R$, where $R$ is the negation of the intersection of the straight line through $P, Q$ and the elliptic curve. When $P = Q$, then take the tangent to the elliptic curve at $P$ instead of a straight line through $P, Q$.

When $P \ne Q$, the formula for $R$ can be found by whacking:

$$ (x_p, y_p) + (x_r, y_r) = (x_q, y_q)\
\text{Let } m = \frac{y_p - y_r}{x_p - x_r}\
x_q = m^2 - x_p - x_r\
y_q = m(x_p - x_r) - y_p\
$$

When $P = Q$, the formula for $R$ is the same, except that $m = \frac{3x_p^2 + a}{2y_p}$.

Note that $P + P + P + P = (P + P) + (P + P)$, which means that fast exponentiation can be used to calculate $nP$ in logarithemtic time.

Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange

The Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange algorithm allows two parties to share a key even without a secure communications channel.

  • Alice and Bob agree on a large prime $p$, and a primitive root $g$ of $p$.
  • Alice chooses a secret integer $a$, and Bob chooses a secret integer $b$.
  • Alice sends $g^a \pmod p$ to Bob, Bob sends $g^b \pmod p$ to Alice.
  • Alice computes $s = (g^b)^a \pmod p = g^{ab} \pmod p$. Bob computes $s = (g^a)^b \pmod p = g^{ab} \pmod p$.

$s = g^{ab} \pmod p$ is the secret key held by both Alice and Bob. Note that any eavesdropper will know $p, g, g^a$ and $g^b$. However, there is currently no known polynomial algorithm for solving the discrete logarithm problem, so attackers cannot reverse $a$ and $b$. They also cannot get $g^{ab}$ from $g^a, g^b$.

Making use of primitive roots is the standard, but not only way to do Diffie-Hellman. In fact, there is a generalized version of Diffie-Hellman:

  • Alice and Bob pick a finite cyclic group $G$ of order $n$ and a generating element $g$ in $G$, where $G$ is written multiplicatively.
  • Alice picks a natural number $a$, $1 < a < n$, and sends $g^a$ to Bob.
  • Bob picks a natural number $b$, $1 < b < n$, and sends $g^b$ to Alice.
  • Alice computes $s = (g^b)^a$. Bob computes $s = (g^a)^b$.

The group $G$ must satisfy the condition that there is no efficient algorithm for computing $g^{ab}$ given $g, g^a$ and $g^b$. A variant of this is Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman.
